Stop 1. "Bull," The Southern White Rhino




Bull, the Toronto Zoo's oldest Southern White Rhino

Life in Crisis: Schad Gallery of Biodiversity

Level 2, Centre Block

Meet "Bull", the Toronto Zoo's oldest Southern White Rhino. He died at the age of 45 and, since February 2009, has been standing tall at the entrance to the ROM's Schad Gallery of Biodiversity.

Why It's Iconic

White rhinos are the second-largest land animal, measuring 11 feet long and weighing 4,500 pounds. This species was brought back from the brink of extinction in the 1990s, but is in serious trouble again due to poaching for its horns.

Stop 2. Coral Reef Tank




Coral Reef

Life in Crisis: Schad Gallery of Biodiversity

Level 2, Centre Block

Walk to the right of the rhino and go straight past the bison and polar bear, walking underneath whales and sharks suspended from the ceiling, to the Coral Reef Tank.

This Coral Reef represents a vital underwater ecosystem in the Indo-Pacific region. It demonstrates the balance between fish and coral, and how they both survive together from the nutrients of a healthy ocean.

Cool Fact

Often mistaken as a plant, the coral is actually an ever-growing colony of invertebrates.

Stop 3. Kroyer's Deep Sea Angler Fish




The Deep Sea Angler Fish

Life in Crisis: Schad Gallery of Biodiversity

Level 2, Centre Block

Keep going further into the gallery till you come across a central display with a very long fish. Right below the elongated fish (known as the Crested Oarfish) is our friend, the Kroyer's Deep Sea angler fish.

The Deep Sea Angler Fish is found 3000 meters below the ocean's surface. It's very difficult to survive in these dark, cold conditions, especially as there are few prey species at those depths. The large mouth and long, sharp teeth of the Angler Fish help capture prey two times longer than itself!

Fun Fact

The name Angler Fish comes from the fishing-rod like antenna that hangs over its mouth. The little flap of tissue at the end of the rod is bio-luminescent, meaning it projects light to attract its prey.

Stop 4. Komodo Dragon




A Komodo dragon skeleton

Life in Crisis: Schad Gallery of Biodiversity

Level 2, Centre Block

Next to the angler fish display and to its right is where you'll find the ferocious Komodo dragon skeleton, posturing alongside the largest flower in the world.

The Komodo dragon carries the formidable reputation of being a fearsome predator. It is the world's largest and most dangerous living lizard, found only in Indonesia near the island of Komodo.

Cool Fact

The Komodo dragon's saliva contains over 50 strains of bacteria, ensuring that any creature that escapes its jaws won't escape for long. Its prey includes birds, invertebrate animals and mammals, but the dragon's food of choice is deer.

Stop 5. Moustached Bats




The ROM's Bat Cave

Life in Crisis: Schad Gallery of Biodiversity

Level 2, Centre Block

Go around the case (no, we're not making you walk around in circles), and take the walkway to your right, just past the cinnamon bear, towards the entrance to the Bat Cave.

The Moustached Bat is a grey-coloured bat found primarily in the caves of Jamaica and Central and South America. It gets its name from the stiff hairs lining its upper lip.

Cool Fact

If you find yourself surrounded by these furry mammals, don't panic! Unlike some of their relatives who also eat meat and fruit, these bats only eat insects.
